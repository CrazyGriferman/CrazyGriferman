-Mania

meaning

madness

words

1.kleptomania
2.dipsomaniac
3.megalomaniac
4.egomaniac

Kleptomania

Meaning

A mental illness in which a person has a strong desire to steal things

Derived from

Klepto- ➡️ kleptein (Greek word) ➡️ to steal

-mania ➡️ madness

How to use the word

Even though kleptomania is often the butt of jokes, it’s actually a serious mental illness, often associated with mood disorders, anxiety disorders, eating disorders, and substance abuse.

Dipsomaniac

Meaning

A person with an extreme and uncontrollable desire for alcohol

Derived from

dipsa (Greek noun) ➡️ thirst
-mania ➡️ madness

How to use the word

Some experts distinguish between an alcoholic and a dipsomaniac, reserving dipsomaniac for someone involved in frequent episodes of binge drinking.

Megalomaniac

Meaning

A mental disorder marked by feelings of great personal power and importance

Derived from

megalo- ➡️ large
-mania ➡️ madness

How to use the word

Megalomaniac is generally thrown around as an insult and rarely refers to real mental illness

Egomaniac

Meaning

Someone who is extremely self-centered and ignores the problems and concerns of others

Derived from

ego- ➡️ I(in Latin) ➡️ sense of self-worth
-mania ➡️ madness

How to use the word

The word egomaniac is thrown around by lots of people who don’t mean much more by it than blowhard or know-it-all

Psych-

Meaning

breath, life, soul

Words

1.psyche
2.psychedelic
3.psychosomatic
4.psychotherapist

Psyche

Meaning

soul, personality, mind

Derived from

psyche (in Greek mythology) ➡️ a beautiful princess who fell in love with Eros, god of love, and went through terrible trials before being allowed to marry him
psych- ➡️ soul

How to use the word

In English, psyche often sounds less spiritual than soul, less intellectual than mind, and more private than personality.

Psychedelic

Meaning

(1) of or relating to a drug (eg. LSD) that produces abnormal and often extreme mental effects
(2) imitating the effects of psychedelic drugs

Derived from

psych- ➡️ mind
deloun (Greek) ➡️ to make visible, reveal

How to use the word

Psychedelics are now used to treat anxiety in patients with cancer, and are being tested in the treatment of such serious conditions as severe depression, alcoholism, and drug addiction

Psychosomatic

Meaning

caused by mental or emotional problems rather than by physical illness

Derived from

psych- ➡️ mind
soma ➡️ body

How to use the word

Psychosomatic suggests the link between mind and body

Psychotherapist

Meaning

one who treats mental or emotional disorder or related bodily ills by psychological means

Derived from

psych- ➡️mind, soul
therapy

Cept

Meaning

to take, seize

Words

1.reception
2.intercept
3.perceptible
4.susceptible

Reception

Meaning

(1) the act of receiving

(2) a social gathering where guests are formally welcomed

Derived from

cept ➡️ to take, seize

How to use the word

When a new novel receives good reviews, we say it has met with a good critical reception.

Intercept

Meaning

to stop, seize, or interrupt (something or someone) before arrival

Derived from

inter- ➡️ between
-cept ➡️ to take, seize

How to use the word

Today, when communications are generally electronic, an intercepted e-mail isn’t actually stopped, but simply read secretly by a third party

Perceptible

Meaning

noticeable or able to be felt by the senses

Derived from

per- ➡️ through
cept ➡️ to take, seize

How to use the word

The word refers to whatever can be taken in through the senses.

Susceptible

Meaning

(1) open to some influence; responsive

(2) able to be submitted to an action or process

Derived from

sus- ➡️ up
cept ➡️ to take, seize

How to use the word

With its prefix sus-, “up,” susceptible refers to something or someone that “takes up” or absorbs like a sponge

Fin-

Meaning

“end” or “boundary”

Words

1.confine
2.definitive
3.finite
4.infinitesimal

Confine

Meaning

(1) to keep (someone or something) within limits
(2) to hold (someone) in a location

Derived from

con- ➡️ with or together
fin- ➡️ end or boundary

How to use the word

Someone confined to a bedroom or a wheelchair is too ill or disabled to be anywhere else.
A person under “house arrest” is confined to his or her house by the government.
At a business meeting, the discussion may be confined to a single topic.

Definitive

Meaning

(1) authoritative and final
(2) specifying perfectly or precisely

Derived from

de- ➡️ completely
fine- ➡️ end or boundary

How to use the word

A definitive answer is usually a strong yes or no
A definitive biography contains everything we’ll ever need to know about someone.

Finite

Meaning

having definite limits

Derived from

fin- ➡️ end or boundary

How to use the word

Finite is mostly used in scientific writing, often with the meaning “definitely measurable”

Infinitesimal

Meaning

extremely or immeasurably or endlessly small

Derived from

infinitus (Latin) ➡️ infinite
-esimus ➡️ ordinal word-forming element

-Ject

Meaning

to throw or hurl

Words

1.interject
2.conjecture
3.projection
4.trajectory

Interject

Meaning

to interrupt a conversation with a comment or remark

Derived from

inter ➡️ between
-ject ➡️ to throw or hurl

How to use the word

For most of the word’s history, the only thins that have been interjected have been comments dropped suddenly into a conversation.

Conjecture

Meaning

to guess

Derived from

con- ➡️ together
-ject ➡️ to throw

How to use the word

Conjecture means literally “to throw together” — that is, to produce a theory by putting together a number of facts.

Projection

Meaning

An estimate of that might happen in the future based on what is happening now

Derived from

pro- ➡️ forward
-ject ➡️ to throw or hurl

How to use the word

Projection has various meanings, but what they all have in common is that something is sent out or forward.
The meaning we focus on here is the one used by businesses and governments.Most projections of this kind are estimates of a company’s sales or profits— something in the future

Trajectory

Meaning

the curved path that an object makes in space, or that a thrown object follows as it rises and falls to earth

Derived from

trans ➡️ across
ject ➡️ to throw, hurl

How to use the word

The word is used most often in physics and engineering, but not always; we can also say, for example, that the trajectory of a whole life may be set in a persons’s youth, or that a new book traces the long trajectory of the French empire.

Tract-

Meaning

to drag or draw

Words

1.traction
2.retract
3.protracted
4.intractable

Traction

Meaning

the friction that allows a moving thing to move over a surface without slipping

Derived from

tract- ➡️ to drag or draw

Retract

Meaning

(1) to pull back (something) into something larger
(2) to take back (something said or written)

Derived from

re- ➡️ back
-tract ➡️ to drag or draw

How to use the word

Thousands of citizens were forced to publicly retract their “wrong” ideas by the Soviet government in the 1930s and the Chinese government in the 1960s.

Protracted

Meaning

Drawn out, continued, or extended

Derived from

pro- ➡️ forward
-tract ➡️ to drag or draw

How to use the word

Protracted usually applies to something drawn out forward in time.
A protracted strike may cripple a company; a protracted rainy spell may rot the roots of vegetables.

Intractable

Meaning

not easily handled, led, taught, or controlled.

Derived from

in- ➡️ negative prefix
-tract ➡️ to drag or draw

How to use the word

The word may describe both people and conditions.
A cancer patient may suffer intractable pain that doctors are unable to treat.An intractable alcoholic goes back to the bottle immediately after “drying out”.

Duc/Duct

Meaning

to lead

Words

1.conducive
2.deduction
3.induce
4.seduction

Conducive

Meaning

tending to promote, encourage, or assist; helpful

Derived from

con- ➡️ with or together
duc ➡️ to lead

How to use the word

Notice that conducive is almost always followed by to
Something conducive “leads to” a desirable result.

Deduction

Meaning

(1) subtraction
(2) the reaching of a conclusion by reasoning

Derived from

de- ➡️ down
duc ➡️ to lead

How to use the word

Deduction also means “reasoning,” and particularly reasoning based on general principles to produce specific findings.

Induce

Meaning

(1) persuade, influence
(2) bring about

Derived from

in- ➡️ in, into, on ,upon
duc ➡️ to lead

How to use the word

Inducing is usually gentle persuasion.
Notice that induct and induction are somewhat different from induce and inducement, though they come from the identical roots.

Seduction

Meaning

(1) temptation to sin, especially temptation to sexual intercourse
(2) attraction or charm

Derived from

se- ➡️ aside
duc ➡️ to lead

How to use the word

Seduction also takes less physical forms.
Advertisements constantly try to seduce us into buying products we hadn’t even known existed.

Sequ-

Meaning

to follow

Words

1.sequential
2.subsequent
3.consequential
4.non sequitur

Sequential

Meaning

(1) arranged in order or in a series
(2) following in a series

Derived from

sequ- ➡️ to follow

How to use the word

Things in sequence, or regular order, are arranged sequentially.
Sequential courses in college must follow each other in the proper order, just like sequential tasks or steps

Subsequent

Meaning

following in time, order, or place; later

Derived from

sub- ➡️ below
sequ- ➡️ to follow

How to use the word

As the definition states, subsequent can refer to time, order, or place.But subsequently, as in “I subsequently learned the real stroy,” simply means “later”

Consequential

Meaning

(1) resulting
(2) important

Derived from

con- ➡️ with or together
sequ- ➡️ to follow

How to use the word

Something consequential follows or comes along with something else.
The “resulting” meaning of consequential is usually seen in legal writing.
Normally consequential means “significant” or “important,” and it’s especially used for events that will produce large consequences, or results.

non sequitur

Meaning

A statement that does not follow logically from anything previously said.

Derived from

non sequitur (in Latin) ➡️ it does not follow

How to use the word

It was Aristotle who identified the non sequitur as one of the basic fallacies of logic—that is, one of the ways in which a person’s reasoning may go wrong. For Aristotle, the non sequitur is usually a conclusion that doesn’t actually result from the reasoning and evidence presented.
